Food System
Agricultural systems account for about one-third of all human-made greenhouse gas emissions.
Crippa et al., Nature Food 2021 · IPCC AR6 WGIII
The Claim
Food systems — production, land use change, and supply chains — contribute roughly one-third of global greenhouse gas emissions, making agriculture both a major source and a key lever.
Why It Matters
Agricultural transition is not a peripheral issue but a central requirement for any serious climate strategy. The same land that emits can sequester.

Water Buffering
Each 1% gain in soil organic matter adds roughly 20,000 gallons of water-holding capacity per acre.
USDA NRCS Soil Health · Bryant et al. · TCU SOM model
The Claim
Each percentage point increase in soil organic matter allows the soil to hold approximately 20,000 additional gallons of water per acre — reducing runoff, improving drought resilience, and lowering irrigation demand.
Why It Matters
This makes soil health a water infrastructure question, not just an agricultural one. In drought-prone regions, SOM improvement is a direct climate adaptation tool.

Carbon Storage
A 1% soil organic matter gain stores 5.8 tons of carbon per acre — equal to 21.3 tons CO₂e at the IPCC conversion rate.
TCU SOM-to-C conversion · USDA NRCS · Lal, Science 2004 · IPCC C×3.67 standard
The Claim
A 1% SOM gain stores approximately 5.8 tons of carbon per acre. Using the IPCC atomic weight ratio (C × 3.67 = CO₂e), this equals 21.3 tons CO₂ equivalent — measurable, persistent, and scalable.
Why It Matters
Expressing gains in CO₂e makes the comparison legible to funders, policymakers, and the public without inflating the underlying science. At scale across millions of acres, the sequestration potential is significant.

Of global GHG
Food System
Some estimates place food and land-use emissions closer to 24%, making agriculture the hardest sector to decarbonize.
Food & Land-Use Coalition · Shukla et al., IPCC 2019
The Range
Estimates range from ~21% to 37% depending on scope — production only, full supply chain, land-use change, or food waste. TCU uses ~1/3 as a conservative synthesis, with 24% as the production-focused lower bound.
Why It Matters
Agriculture is uniquely difficult to decarbonize through technology substitution alone. Biological regeneration is the primary available tool — making soil health a climate imperative, not a niche practice.

Contested Claim
The "1% solution" is a consequential public framing — directionally supported by TCU, but it requires careful context.
4 per mille initiative · Minasny et al., GCB 2017
The Claim
The 4 per mille initiative proposes that a 0.4% annual increase in soil carbon globally would theoretically offset all annual fossil fuel CO₂ emissions. TCU supports this as directionally important.
The Contest
Critics note that global uniformity is unrealistic and not all soils sequester at that rate. TCU treats this as an aspirational framing that highlights scale — not a site-specific target.

Time Scale
Without active drawdown, excess atmospheric carbon will continue affecting climate for hundreds to thousands of years.
NOAA Carbon Cycle Research · Archer et al., 2009
The Claim
NOAA carbon cycle research indicates a significant fraction of CO₂ persists for 300–1,000 years, meaning emissions today affect climate centuries from now even if we stop emitting tomorrow.
The Implication
Stopping emissions alone does not remove existing damage. Active drawdown is a separate and necessary task — the atmosphere will not self-correct on any policy-relevant timescale.
